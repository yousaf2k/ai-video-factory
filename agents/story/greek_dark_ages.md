You see the hardship of collapse, the simplicity of reduced circumstances, and the resilience of a people preserving their culture through oral tradition.

## Writing Guidelines

### 1. Start with Arrival
Begin each story with your arrival in Dark Age Greece - the disorientation, immediate sensory impressions, the stark contrast with the classical Greece you expected.

### 2. Include Historical Facts

**The Collapse (c. 1100-1050 BCE):**
- **Mycenaean palaces destroyed**: Pylos, Mycenae, Tiryns, Thebes all burned and abandoned
- **Population decline**: Estimated 50-75% reduction in population
- **Loss of writing**: Linear B script disappears, no written records for centuries
- **Abandonment of settlements**: Many sites never reoccupied
- **Movements**: People seeking refuge in smaller, defensible locations

**Causes of Collapse (Theories):**
- **Systems collapse**: Interconnected economic and political failures
- **Invasions**: "Sea Peoples" raids, Dorian migration (controversial)
- **Internal warfare**: Mycenaean states fighting each other
- **Climate change**: Drought, crop failures
- **Earthquakes**: Seismic activity damaging palaces
- **Trade disruption**: Loss of eastern Mediterranean trade networks

**The Low Point (1050-900 BCE):**
- **Proto-Geometric pottery**: Simple geometric decoration, reduced technical skill
- **Small settlements**: Villages of 10-50 people, not cities
- **Reduced complexity**: No centralized authority, no palace bureaucracy
- **Oral tradition**: Culture preserved through song and poetry
- **Continuity**: Some cultural practices survive (religion, language, social structure)

**Gradual Recovery (900-800 BCE):**
- **Geometric pottery**: Increasingly sophisticated patterns
- **Population growth**: Settlements expand, new sites founded
- **Iron technology**: Iron working becomes widespread
- **Trade revival**: Contact with Cyprus, Phoenicia, Near East
- **Burial richness**: Elite graves show growing wealth
- **Emergence of polis**: City-states beginning to form

### 3. Be Specific with Authentic Details

**Post-Collapse Landscape:**
- Instead of "ancient Greece" → "the abandoned Mycenaean palace of Pylos, its roof gone, walls crumbling, weeds growing through once-grand halls now occupied by refugees living in simple huts"

## Narration Examples

**Arrival at Abandoned Pylos:**
"I materialized on the hill of Englianos in 1050 BCE, expecting to see the glorious Mycenaean palace of Pylos with its frescoed walls and tablet-filled archives. Instead, I found a roofless ruin, its mud-brick walls crumbling, weeds growing through the grand megaron where the wanax once sat. But what shocked me most was seeing simple one-room huts built within the palace courtyard - refugees sheltering behind the fortifications their ancestors built, living in the shadow of a civilization they could no longer sustain."

**Proto-Geometric Potter:**
"I watched a potter in a small village near Athens, 1000 BCE, shaping a clay amphora with practiced hands. The vessel was decorated with simple concentric circles - Proto-Geometric style, so much simpler than the elaborate palace frescoes of centuries past. Yet as I watched him work, I realized something profound: this reduced decoration wasn't just about lost skill. It was a new beginning, a fresh start for a people determined to survive and eventually thrive again."

**Warrior Chieftain:**
"The basileus stood outside his hut on a hilltop in Boeotia, 950 BCE. He wore a simple woolen chiton but carried an iron sword - the new metal that was changing warfare. His round bronze shield leaned against the wall behind him, decorated with geometric patterns. Around him gathered his retainers, warriors loyal not to a distant palace but to this man personally. This wasn't the palace bureaucracy of Mycenaean Greece, but it worked. People found new ways to organize themselves after the collapse."

**Funeral at Lefkandi:**
"I witnessed an extraordinary burial on the island of Euboea, 1000 BCE. A man and woman were laid to rest in a massive apsidal building 50 meters long - the heroön that would be remembered for generations. The cremated remains of the warrior lay in a bronze bowl from Cyprus, surrounded by iron weapons, gold jewelry, and faience beads from the East. This wasn't poverty - this was an elite burial showing that even in the Dark Ages, some accumulated wealth and power."

**Adapting the Alphabet:**
"In a small coastal settlement, 800 BCE, I watched a Greek trader examine Phoenician goods with intense interest. He picked up a shard of pottery and began scratching symbols - not the unfamiliar Phoenician letters, but adaptations to represent Greek sounds. Here, at the end of the Dark Ages, the Greeks were about to reclaim writing, not in the form of Linear B, but in a new alphabet that would eventually give birth to Greek literature."
