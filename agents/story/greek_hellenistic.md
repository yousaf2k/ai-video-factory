- **Cassander**: Controls Macedonia and Greece

**Ptolemaic Egypt (323-30 BCE):**
- **Alexandria**: New capital, Center of learning and culture
- **Library**: Founded by Ptolemy I, expanded by Ptolemy II
- **Museum**: Research institution with salaried scholars
- **Pharos**: Lighthouse built (280 BCE), One of Seven Wonders

**Seleucid Empire (312-63 BCE):**

- **Cities**: New Greek cities founded throughout the empire
- **Decline**: Civil wars, Parthian expansion, Roman conquest
- **End**: Pompey annexes Syria (63 BCE)

**Antigonid Macedonia (276-168 BCE):**

**Coming of Rome:**

## Narration Examples

**Death of Alexander:**
"I stood in the Babylonian palace in 323 BCE as Alexander the Great died at age 32. The conqueror who had remade the world lay on his deathbed, his fevered body too weak even to lift his hand. When his generals asked 'To whom do you leave your empire?' he whispered 'To the strongest' - and with those words, the Hellenistic world was born. The empire would fragment into warring kingdoms, Greek culture would spread and change, and the independent city-states of Classical Greece would exist no more. I watched as the generals began plotting, knowing the Wars of the Diadochi would come soon."

**Library of Alexandria:**
"The Great Library of Alexandria in 250 BCE was the most extraordinary place I had ever seen. Scrolls from Greece, Egypt, Babylon, Persia, India - half a million works of human knowledge. Jewish scholars translated the Torah into Greek (the Septuagint) so Diaspora Jews could read it. Euclid sat with geometry students, demonstrating proofs that would still be taught two thousand years later. Eratosthenes measured the shadow of a well at noon and calculated Earth's circumference with an error of only 15%. Callimachus cataloged every scroll in the library. This was the internet of the ancient world - all human knowledge in one place, supported by Ptolemaic patronage."

**Archimedes' Claw:**
"In Syracuse in 212 BCE, I watched Archimedes demonstrate his latest invention to King Hieron. The Claw was a massive iron crane on the city walls that could lift entire Roman ships out of the water and capsize them. 'With this,' the old man said simply, 'we will break the Roman blockade.' I saw it work during the siege - Roman quinqueremes seized by the iron claw, lifted high, and dropped to shatter on the rocks below. The Romans withdrew in terror, believing Syracuse was defended by the gods. But it was only Greek genius - the same mind that discovered pi and the principle of buoyancy."

**Battle of Actium:**
"The Bay of Actium on September 2, 31 BCE was where the Hellenistic world ended. Octavian's fleet faced off against Mark Antony and Cleopatra's combined forces. I watched from a Roman ship as Antony's flagship with its distinctive purple sail tried to break through. Cleopatra's Egyptian squadron followed. But Octavian's admiral Agrippa had positioned his forces perfectly. When Cleopatra's ships turned to flee, Antony followed - abandoning his fleet. The battle became a rout. I saw the future: Rome would dominate the Mediterranean, the Hellenistic kingdoms would fall, and Cleopatra would commit suicide in Alexandria a year later. An age was ending."

**Stoic Porch:**
"The Painted Porch (Stoa Poikile) in Athens, 300 BCE, was where Zeno taught Stoicism to an international audience - Greeks, Romans, Egyptians, Syrians. 'Virtue is the only good,' he said simply. 'External circumstances cannot harm the wise person.' I watched a Cilician merchant debate a Macedonian soldier about whether wealth mattered. A Babylonian astronomer questioned Zeno about providence. The philosophy that would influence Roman emperors and Christian theologians was being born in this international marketplace of ideas - a response to a world where traditional Greek city-states had lost their independence to vast kingdoms."
